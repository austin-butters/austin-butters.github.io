My Research

What is Emotional Intelligence?

Emotional intelligence (EI) is also known as "EQ" for "Emotional Quotient. It is similar to IQ (Intelligence Quotient), but IQ and EQ focus on different skills. Emotional Intelligence is the grouping of many skills, but to summarize, it relates to the ability to recognise the emotions of both yourself and others, and the ability to understand, regulate, control, and use emotions effectively.

Having a high EQ means it's easier to regulate your own emotions, and to understand the way your own mind works. This can be very useful when it comes to managing your own work, learning, relationships, and overall happiness. People with high EQ are often more motivated, perform higher, and work harder. This is not because they are inherently better workers or smarter people, but because they understand how to best manage themselves, their energy, and their motivation for work. They are also more empathetic and better able to understand and interact with other people and their emotions, which can lead to better relationships and human connection.

Peter Salovey and John Mayer (psychologists and professors), define Emotional Intelligence as "accurately perceiving emotion, using emotions to facilitate thought, understanding emotion, and managing emotion."

Studies and research on Emotional Intelligence are controversial and there isn't a huge, evidence based scientific agreement. That aside, it is generally agreed that emotional intelligence, and the skills that it consists of, are important, life-improving skills to develop.

Emotional Intelligence is comprised of many skills, which can most noteably be grouped into the following:

  1. The ability to understand and explain what other people are feeling
  2. The ability to understand and explain what you are feeling yourself
  3. Self awareness of your own personal strengths and limitations
  4. Self-confidence and self-acceptance
  5. The ability to forgive, both your own mistakes and those of others
  6. Strong curiosity about many subjects, particularly relating to other people
  7. Feeling empathy towards others, being sensitive to their feelings as well as your own
  8. The ability to reason with and manage your own emotions in difficult or stressful situations

How is EQ different from IQ?

Both IQ and EQ are simply measurements of how much someone excels and certain skills. IQ and EQ are different groupings of skills. IQ is the more universally recognised and used measurement, and there is far more research done on IQ than EQ. IQ has a more standardised measurement system (for example, the average person has an IQ score of around 100), but EQ is harder to measure, and measurement systems are more disputed. There is a lot less scientific agreement on the EQ.

IQ focuses on cognitive ability, such as analytical skills, pattern recognition, and problem solving. It can be thought of as a measurement of overall brainpower. EQ focuses on a different grouping of skills, which relate to emotions and interpersonal relationships. These skills include empathy, understanding emotions, communication, self-awareness, and so on.

Why is EQ important?

People with high EQ tend to be better at managing themselves, staying positive, staying motivated, and many more things. They often have better mental health and higher overall happiness.

People with high EQ also have greater self awareness, meaning they recognise and understand their own emotions, and the factors that contribute to the way they are feeling. By understanding these factors, we can understand outselves better, and be aware of the way we respond to our own emotions. This means that people with higher EQ have higher self control. Self control can be summarised as the ability to think before reacting to situations. By understanding why we are feeling the way we are feeling, we can reason with our own emotions. For example, rather than taking foolish action because we are feeling stressed or angry, we can recognise what we are feeling a certain emotion, analyse what factors causing emotions, and think about how best to respond before taking action.

Empathy for others is also very important. With higher EQ comes a greater understanding of how our actions and behavior can influnce others' emotions, for better or for worse. This creates a greater sense of empathy, which improves relationships.

All of the benefits that come with high EQ have been shown to improve both personal and professional lives. It improves the quality of relationships, the quality of work, our level of motivation, and our overall mental wellbeing.

Question (From the student handbook):

Can you bring to mind a time when you felt like you were in an optimal state of being? Where your mind was bright and clear, and there was a sense of capability? When weren't you being trashed by your internal judge or superego? A sense of stability and grounding? Aware of your strengths and limitations? An optimal state of being! If we want one word for what we're aiming for here, it's "optimal".

I can't recall a time when I've felt all of these things at once. But I can certainly say there are times when I feel better than others. The most signinficant influence is when I'm calm, rested, energised, and feeling physically well in general. I find that for me, my my physical state usually comes first, and if my physical state isn't good, my mental state often isn't either. I've learnt not to trust any negative thoughts I have about things when I'm on less than five hours of sleep, sick, dehydrated, or anything else that compromises my physical health. This is a lot, but I've found that any time I have negative thoughts about myself, or am feeling bad in general, there's usually a physical factor involved as well.

When I'm feeling great physically, I usually feel better emotionally as well. When I'm not one hundred percent, I find that taking the time to relax and simply "do nothing" and just exist really helps me to clear my head and remove stress.

Reflections - Mindfulness

Do you have any biases for or against mindfulness? If so, can you describe them?

It's not something I've done a lot of, as I often feel really busy and like I don't have enough time to just "do nothing." I don't know a lot about mindfulness since I haven't done much of it, but it's definately possible I'm underestimating it's effects and usefulness. I tend to think that mindfulness may help me focus, but not enough to make up for the working time I lose by practicing mindfulness.

How did you find the overall experience?

I actually really enjoyed it, I was still tired and a little stressed after last sprint (though I'm trying to prioritise and schedule better in this sprint and future ones), so it was good to just relax and stop working or being busy, even if just for a few minutes.

What's your daily habit commitment for mindfulness?

I will practice mindfulness before each study session where I engage with DevAcademy's curriculum, I will also do a mindfulness/meditation session every day after my morning routines (breakfast, showering, etc), before I go about with my day. I've only done one mindfulness session so far but I did enjoy it, so assuming it starts helping me significantly, I intend to continue this habbit into the future, after DevAcademy finishes.

Reflections - Listening and Looping

What was it like to be fully listened to?

Normally in conversation, you wouldn't go two minutes with only one person talking, so initially it felt unnatural and a little weird. I found it became more naturall over time, and It was good to talk about what was difficult or frustrating for me. It definately helped me to connect with Sean who I had just met for the first time in person.

What was it like to fully listen?

In most conversations, I tend to be the less talkative one, so the not talking aspect of it wasn't too hard. What was different was the listening. In most conversations, I'm half listening to the other person, and half thinking. In this case, I wasn't thinking about my response because I had to fully listen. I think it was a very good excersize because it helped me to get to know the other person much faster than I normally would have. Initially it's a bit stressful talking about what's difficult for me, and what I'm struggling with because I kind of think it makes me sound "less smart", but when you both do the excersise you realise you're usually in the same place.
