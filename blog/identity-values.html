Sprint 1 Human Skills - Identity and Values

29 March, 2025

Explain a situation where you have made an ethical decision. Discuss how you weighed up the values involved in that decision, the decision you made, and how you reflect on the decision now.

This isn't such an obvious "ethical decision", but I think it's important and very relevant to me, this course, and the other questions here. I started my first job working at KFC when I was sixteen, and I continued to work there throughout the rest of my time at high school. As an extremely hard worker who was working long hours at such a young age during high school exams, I quickly became one of Management's most valued team members and made good friendships with many of my coworkers. Initially I planned to take a full gap year after finishing high school and study film at university in 2026. I quickly realised that the gap year was going to be unfulfilling, because I no longer was making any progress, and my job at KFC was a big time commitment that provided financial stability but no progress. It also provided no fulfilment as the job had no alignment with my personal values, it was just a time-for-money trade. Because I value personal development I decided I had to find a way to improve my skillset/career or myself in 2025, and DevAcademy seemed like the perfect opportunity. This was a difficult decision to make because normally I overthink decisions and like to spend way too much time weighing up all the pros, cons and consequences. This is what I consider to be one of my weaknesses, and I had recently decided to try and approach life with a "good enough" mindset (more on that in the following questions). I realised I didn't have to remain at KFC to continue those friendships. If the friendships were truely strong enough, I'd make the effort to maintain them, and so far I still am with my closest friends from there. I'm now making progress and developing new skills daily. I'm feeling far more satisfied with life in terms of both the big picture, and my day-to-day life. This was a decision I am very happy with now, but was hard to make at the time because it required me to take a risk without knowing everything. I'm so glad I made this decision.

Describe how your culture and the people around you have influenced your values and identity.

The most significant aspects of my values and identity come from my family. Growing up, I was taught by my parents to be a hard worker, and I developed this skill by doing lots of chores around the house, working hard on personal projects, and eventually making significant contributions to building the house I currently live in with my family (which was not something we knew how to do, and taught me to be persistent.) I take a lot of pride in my work ethic, and my ability to follow through with doing the things that I commit to doing. I've also had a lot of artistic influence in my life through media (e.g. movies, music, etc) and friends and family members who have their own creative endeavors. Because of this I highly value personal creativity and expression. My identity is significantly shaped by my family and friends, so I value the strong relationships I have with others, and nurturing them. I have had many very different "phases" of my life and because I tend to be optemistic, I am always excited about what changes the future has for me. I value self improvement and personal development, which is a major reason for enrolling in DevAcademy.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

I'm naturally a logical/mathematical/methodical thinker, which has made it easy to pick up the basics of this course in the first week. The results of the "Strength testing" website linked in the student handbook said that my greatest stremgth is Judgement. Specifically, this means that I'm very good at weighing information, and I like to know all the facts, to make the best possible, unbiased decision. This matches my "INFJ" result from the Sixteen Personalities Test. Another one of my strengths is my strong work ethic; INFJs are known to be good at taking action on their goals. I have persistance and stamina, and the ability to work on difficult tasks for a long time, until the whole thing is thorougly completed to a very high standard. I'm good at disciplining myself to stay motivated, and sometimes I like to think I'm a little insane because I don't know many other people as "stubbornly motivated" as myself. By "stubbornly motivated", I mean I often refuse to go back on what I've told myself I will do. When I commit do doing something, I stick at it until it's thoroughly done (as previously mentioned in the question about "values and identity"). I'm very good at looking at both the big picture and the small details, and seeing how they all come together.

Evaluate your limitations in terms of your learning and career development. How might these affect your learning journey?

My biggest weaknesses and limitations come from the overuse of my strengths. Because I like to be thorough I tend to be a perfectionist, which often means that I don't make as much progress as I should. Sometimes this means I haven't done things at all if I can't do them perfectly. Sometimes I'm a "judger" to a fault, meaning I wait until I have all the information and am fully prepared to make a change in my life. Sometimes this waiting takes too long and I don't end up taking action at all. Recently I have tried to approach life with a "good enough" attitude, being willing to take action even if I'm don't feel fully ready. Things don't have to be perfect, just "good enough". A quote I like is that "Life rewards action, not overthinking. Take action first, then use the learning to recalibrate after." Normally my overthinking can prevent progress but recently I have been working on this, and this is what has allowed me to quit my previous job at KFC and enroll in this course. It's exciting and I look forward to the future. I will need to work to maintain this mindset in order to make the most of the learning in this course.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

An example of a time I was trying to work productively with others was in high school, when me and a group of friends were making a short film for our film class. As all of us were film fans, we took the project very seriously and invested a lot of our personal time and money into the project. We spent time planning, but once we were actually shooting the film, we realised just how hard working as a team was. The producer (who is in charge of everything logistics-wise) was disorganised and consistently showed up late. I was the director, and I was distracted by other things in my life and often in a world of my own. The producer was more experienced than I was, and as the two of us had slightly differing artistic visions, the producer would often override what I was wanting, giving instructions of his own. The whole production got very messy and I had to sort out a lot of the mess left behind by the producer. The film set was slowly falling apart so I organised a meeting with all of the primary crew, and we discussed what was and wasn't working with the production, what we were and weren't enjoying, and possible solutions to the problem. We realised that we had never perfectly defined our roles and as their weren't many of us, the roles were constantly overlapping. We came to the eventual solution to swap roles, meaning I would produce, and the previous producer would direct. This worked for the most part, as I was distracted creatively but was still able to sort out logistical things, and my friend was now able to direct properly, which is what he was trying to do anyway. The film set ran far more efficiently after this, but unfortunately we weren't able to finish the film as we had already gone too far over schedule before making that change. If I were to do this again, I'd create a more solid version plan from the beggining, making sure to fully define our roles and the workflow we would use to create the film. In that workflow, I would also include a process to go through any time their were conflicts, so that we could address and resolve them as fast as possible, and get back on track with our project. This could include a voting system, a chain of command, or any other way of resolving conflicts.
