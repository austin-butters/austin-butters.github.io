Sprint 5 Human Skills - My Updated Learning Plan

24 April, 2025 (Updated)

Sprint 5 is the last full sprint for DevAcademy foundations. To prepare for bootcamp, I'm reflecting on my learning plan throughout foundations and updating it in preparation for bootcamp.

Reassess your Learning Plan

"The pressures and challenges of Bootcamp will be different from those in Foundations and the type of help and support available to you. We will revisit similar questions to those that you answered when first formulating your Learning Plan, but now with a particular focus on learning goals and objectives while on Bootcamp. Take some time to reflect on these questions and record your answers in a document."

What is your long term goal and/or career pathway?

"What are you hoping to use the skills you gain during this course for? What impact would you like to make once you leave Dev Academy? Do you have a destination in mind?"

It remains mostly the same as when I first made my learning plan, but I will add some more details. After this course I have a potential oportunity to do some work for Volition Solutions LTD, which is an app that allows disabled people to record their preferences so they can receive the best possible support. If I do get to do this work, it'll have a great social impact. Of course, currently that is a possibility, nothing is definite.

The other thing I've noticed is that I've really enjoyed all of the projects so far, so I'd like to think that I might work on some sort of personal project for something I'm passionate about, if the right idea strikes.

I'll also be looking for general development work of course, but as of the current moment I don't have strong opinions about exactly what kind it would be.

What do you think your biggest strengths and limitations will be in Bootcamp?

"Re-evaluate your strengths and limitations from Sprint 2. Has your idea of these changed throughout Foundations? Have you discovered any new ones during this course? How do you think these will help/hinder you during Bootcamp?"

My biggest strength will probably be keeping up with the technical content. I totally expect to be confused a lot (or even most) of the time, but I expect that I'll do fine keeping up with everything. So far I've done a very good job of keeping up (and often even finishing the week's work before the weekend), so I know I'm good at pulling through with deadlines, and I'm pretty happy with my comprehension and ability to learn technical things.

I've noticed more and more during foundations that I'm really bad at sticking to the basics of looking after myself (consistent sleep, staying hydrated, eating well), I tend to do less than is ideal, as long as I know I'll still be able to finish the work. I would have liked to think that before DevAcademy this was mostly just because I didn't have any reason to do these things because I wasn't studying or doing particularly important work. I've since come to realise that it's more of a mindset I have that keeps me doing that (mostly with the unhealthy sleep schedule), so this will be my biggest weakness going into bootcamp. It's not directly learning related, but the better I do at these things, the better I am able to learn. I'm hoping that the more rigid structure of bootcamp will motivate (or force) me to start developing better habits and routines with this, that I can retain after the course.

What do you think your biggest non-technical challenge at Bootcamp will be?

"While you will face many technical challenges on Bootcamp, this is not what we would like you to consider in this plan. Non-technical challenges might include staying on top of the material, working in groups, speaking in front of the group, keeping focused for nine weeks, etc."

My biggest non-technical challenge will be working in groups. I need to improve my teamwork skills and I hope to do so in bootcamp. This will be particularly difficult for me when people have differing ideas to me and also when working on different schedules (both of which are to be expected in the workforce so are important skills).

What non-technical skills - human skills - would you like to see developed in yourself while at Dev Academy?

"At Dev Academy, we have a significant focus on human skills and improving yourself. Based on your strengths/limitations, what are some areas for development you would like to work on? For example, this could be communication, teamwork within groups, pairing with others, giving and receiving feedback, developing some leadership skills, or improving your self-awareness and reflective skills."

A big goal for me in bootcamp regarding human skills is to improve my communication and teamwork skills for group projects. I'm usually very good at working hard on personal projects but not always the best at working collaboratively, especially when differing opinions are involved. I know that I need to work on my ability to let others contribute to my projects, even if they have different ways of doing things, and I need to not get hung up on minor details as a result of the fact that I'm still learning.

My biggest non-technical challenge in bootcamp will be improving said skills.

What are your expectations from the Bootcamp team?

"What does support look like to you? Keep in mind, this may not be the same as the Bootcamp team's expectations, but it is helpful to voice these expectations."

I expect facilitators to be respectful and understanding of struggles/issues I may have during the course, and I expect to be able to communicate with facilitators regularly about this. For me, ideally support just involves being able to check in with someone who can guide me in the right direction (not giving me answers or anything, but enough advice to keep me learning on my own).

What are your expectations of yourself on Bootcamp

"Based on your knowledge of Bootcamp, how and where will you seek help in a timely way? Write down a commitment to how you will manage your workload in this programme. This should include - an explanation of how you will manage yourself to work productively and safely with other learners, facilitators and industry/community representatives."

My expectations for myself are to give it my all. I'll do my best to maintain a positive attitude and be respectful and friendly to other students and facilitators. I will do my best to look after myself in my personal life so that I am in the best position to learn.

When have run into problems or blockers that I can't solve with research or just thinking about it, I'll first reach out to other students for help, then facilitators. When seeking help, I will do so in a respectful manner, and make sure I understand the problem I am having to the best of my ability so I can clearly communicate it to the person I'm seeking help from. I usually find that I learn best when I've solved a problem on my own so I like to think about the problem and research solutions myself first, but I do recognise that bootcamp will be intensive and there may not always be time to solve every problem on my own. For this reason, I'll make sure to reach out for help after I've tried to solve a problem on my own for some time (This amount of time will of course differ, depending on the size of the problem, but generally, I think that 20-30 minutes on a bug or technical issue is reasonable.)

To manage my workload, I'll continue to use the techniques I've previously used. This includes skimming over the week's curriculum beforehand so that I can better plan and schedule my work. I'll continue to use the techniques I previously talked about in sprint 2 (See below on this page), which have already improved my procrasination habit. Additionally, I've found that the two minute anti-procrastination technique works very well for me too. (This technique involves forcing yourself to work for two minutes, allowing yourself to stop after. More often than not, this is enough time to sink in and continue working.) I also expect bootcamp to have a more rigid structure that makes procrastination harder, but it's still important that I manage my procrastination.

As with seeking help, I'll try to make all of my communications clear and respectful with other students, facilitators, and industry/community representatives so that work can be as productive as possible without time being lost to communication.

Sprint 2 Human Skills - My Learning Plan

05 April, 2025

This is my learning plan, from the Human Skills section of Sprint 2. Following my plan should improve my learning.

What is your long term goal or career pathway?

I’m passionate about many different things, and at times have wanted to be an architect, developer, and a filmmaker. I think my strongest passion and most long-standing one is filmmaking, I’ve always loved movies and would love to make them myself. But I do realize that there’s not a great way to get into it and make a living off of it until you’re very successful. I’ve decided to pursue development and go to DevAcademy so that I can start a career that will hopefully go great places while I also explore filmmaking. In the future, filmmaking may become a more major pursuit, I’d have to see how it goes.

A description of your strengths and limitations when it comes to learning

My strengths and weaknesses are kind of the same thing. I’m VERY thorough. This means that when I’m interested in something, I like to deep dive into things and learn everything I can about a particular subject. This becomes a weakness when I’m to thorough, because it makes me a perfectionist and I don’t like moving on if my knowledge on the current topic feels incomplete.

My other weakness is a fairly universal one, procrastination.

What skills (non-technical/human skills) would you like to see developed in yourself while at Dev Academy?

Communication as part of a team. I recognize this is an important skill to develop and is essential for teamwork. I also know that I personally have a lot of room for improvement, because I often come to things with an I’ll-do-it-all-myself-so-it’s-exactly-how-I-want-it attitude. I’ve never worked on a coding project before, but I do have experience working in a team of filmmakers, as filmmaking is a very teamwork-based type of work. When I’ve made short films with friends, the biggest issue we face has always been communication.

A commitment to how you will manage your workload in this program. This should include:

An explanation of how you will manage yourself to work productively and safely with other learners, Facilitators and industry/community representatives

Starting next sprint, to manage myself and my workload, I will skim over the entire week’s curriculum and lay out a schedule for my learning. It may be impossible to follow perfectly if things come up, but having the schedule will help me to make good progress. I’m also going to make sure to weight most of the workload towards the start of the week, so that if/when things do come up, I’ve gotten most of the work out of the way.

When it comes to procrastination, I've realised that I have a specific amount of work to do each week. Every minute of work that I do on Monday is work that I don't have to do on the weekend. By thinking in this way, I've managed to successfully get a lot of work done at the start of the week, even without a close deadline hanging over me. I've also told my friends and family about this approach, and they recognise that my time is valuable so they're holding me accountable for this. Overall, this will help me to be far less stressed by the workload.

Another thing that will help me to stay productive is maintaining my physical health. I'm going to focus on healthy eating and hydration, getting enough sleep and not having caffiene after 2PM.

I will do my best to make all of my communications with other students and with facilitators as clear as possible, to avoid confusion. I’ll also make sure to keep all of the messages respectful. When studying in-person for bootcamp, I will do my best to be friendly and meet everyone I’ll be working with. Socializing with new people is also a human skill that I need to work on, so I’ll make sure to focus on this for bootcamp.

What you will do when things are building up and starting to get to you?

I think I have a pretty good idea of how to deal with this as I have other experience from film projects and school deadlines, so I know how best to manage my own stress. The first thing I’ll do is step back from the work to clear my head, or if there’s not time, I’ll try my best to relax. When studying from home in foundations, that usually means getting out of the house for a while. Next, I’ll come back to the work, and ask myself if it’s good enough. I’m usually far more thorough than I need to be, and most of the time It’s actually already good enough for the level I’m learning at. If my work is still not good enough, I’ll reschedule and create a plan to finish my work by the deadline.

A commitment as to how and where you will seek help in a timely way

When I’m stuck, and I can’t find the problem after looking and researching myself, I’ll first reach out to fellow students on either my home group or the foundations help desk on discord. I think it’s good to reach out to students as well because teaching is also a great way of learning something, so it helps everyone. After that I’ll reach out to a facilitator either on the foundations help desk, my home group, or message them directly, depending on the nature of my problem. In all situations, I’ll make my communication clear using the advice on “Asking For Help” in the student handbook. I don’t know exactly what the learning dynamic in bootcamp is, but I assume I’ll be able to use the same methods in person.

A description of what you expect from the Facilitation team

I expect the Facilitation team to be responsive to questions and problems I may have (mostly with learning the content, but I also expect them to understand if there are unavoidable problems that may affect my ability to participate in the course, of course I will do my best to avoid these kinds of problems.) I expect them to be professional, respectful and understanding, and of course I will be respectful as well.

Any scheduling information such as block-out times when you are committed to other things. For example, you might have whānau commitments like school drop-offs. It's important to plan these in.

I have no other strict, unchanging commitments at the moment. Usually there are one or two things that come up each week, but they’re not consistent things that can be planned on. I’ve made sure that I will always have 12:30PM on Wednesdays free for homegrown meetings.

I also like to have my weekend nights away from work and study for socializing, etc, but that’s really a personal commitment to myself, that I will finish my work by then.
